The Podcast

“An age of extraordinary energy. Of railroads and steel and scientific discovery. Of a civilization inventing the future at a pace the world had never seen.”

Between 1860 and 1913, Germany transformed from a fragmented collection of thirty-nine states into the most productive industrial and scientific civilization on earth. The research university. Aspirin. The internal combustion engine. Synthetic chemistry. X-rays. The modern electrical grid. All German.

The German Century covers this era in full — the statecraft of Bismarck, the wars that unified the nation, the Kaiserreich at its extraordinary height. Serious, narrative history, told in English for the first time.

Hosted by Prof. Josef Ezekiel Von Gieslingen, who has spent fifty years trying to make people care about this period and has finally found a medium that works.

About the Host
Prof. Josef Ezekiel Von Gieslingen
Philadelphia, 2001. His wife insisted on the photograph. He did not want to be there. It was taken in front of a famous American historical landmark. He is a German history professor. Well — close enough.
“I have spent my career studying one of the most extraordinary civilizations in the history of the modern world. Most people have never been told it existed.”

Dr. Josef Ezekiel Von Gieslingen was born in Munich in 1952. He completed his doctorate at the Ludwig Maximilian University in 1979 and taught European history there for fifteen years. In 1993 he came to the United States as a visiting professor at Rutgers University, and later at the Pennsylvania State University. He has lived in America for over thirty years. He speaks English, as his grandson Tyler frequently notes, rather well.

His specialty is the Bismarckian and Wilhelmine eras — the fifty-three years between 1860 and 1913. He has written many books. Twelve people have read them. This podcast is his attempt to reach the other eight billion.

His family has been Bavarian for many centuries. (See the photograph at the bottom of the page.)

A Note from Tyler

“I just heard my grandfather telling fascinating stories. I said: you just keep talking. I will handle everything else.”

My grandfather has been telling these stories his entire life. At the dinner table. On walks. To anyone who would sit still long enough. For thirty years he wrote books about Imperial Germany that, by his own admission, twelve people read.

A few years ago I got him into podcasts. He became obsessed. His walks got longer. My grandmother called me twice to ask if something had happened to him. Nothing had happened. He had just discovered that you could tell history this way — without footnotes, directly to people, in a form they might actually listen to.

He kept saying the same thing: nobody is telling this story properly in English. So I told him: just keep talking the way you always talk. I will build the website, set up the recording, handle everything. He had no idea what any of that meant. He still doesn’t, really.

This is the result. He talks. I do everything else. It is a good arrangement.

— Tyler. Digital marketing. The only person in this operation who has sent an email.

A Family Connection
Ludwig Von Gieslingen
Augsburg, c. 1874.
A Bavarian family, many centuries in this land.
“My family has been Bavarian for many centuries. This is the era they lived through. That is reason enough to tell it properly.”

The Von Gieslingen family has lived in Bavaria for as long as any record exists. They were present for the events described in this podcast — not as statesmen or generals, but as the ordinary Bavarian subjects that history moves around and through.

They watched German unification from the Bavarian side, which means they watched it with complicated feelings. Bavaria was Catholic. Bavaria had affinities with Austria that it did not share with Protestant Prussia. They did not simply walk into the new Germany — they were maneuvered there.

Prof. Von Gieslingen has spent fifty years trying to understand what they lived through. This podcast is the result of that effort.
